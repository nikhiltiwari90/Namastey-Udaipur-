# Customer Research & Personas
## Namastey Udaipur
*Author: Nikhil Tiwari | Product Discovery | 2015*

---

## Research Methodology

### Primary Research

**Method 1: Observational Research**
Spent 3 weekends at high-traffic tourist areas in Udaipur (City Palace, Fateh Sagar Lake, Jagdish Temple, Old City Bazaar) observing tourist behavior patterns.

**Key observations:**
- Tourists from South India consistently clustered together; separated from North Indian groups
- Frequent pattern: Tourist staring at a sign, looking confused, then moving on without engaging
- Multiple instances of tourists asking locals for help, locals responding in Hindi, tourists not understanding
- Tamil/Bengali groups consistently checking phones (WhatsApp, likely asking home network for recommendations) rather than using travel apps
- At restaurants: many South Indian tourists walked past Rajasthani restaurants and sought out South Indian joints they found through word-of-mouth only

**Method 2: Informal User Interviews**
Conducted 14 informal interviews with tourists at chai shops and near popular sites. Recruited by approaching groups speaking South Indian languages.

**Screener:** "Are you visiting Udaipur? Is this your first time? What's been most difficult?"

**Key quotes (paraphrased, translated):**

> "We asked Google Maps for restaurants but all the reviews are by people who eat differently from us. We don't know which reviews to trust." — Tamil traveler, group of 4

> "My parents wanted to eat familiar food for at least one meal a day. We spent two hours on day one finding a South Indian restaurant. The app [MakeMyTrip] was useless." — 30-year-old traveler with elderly parents, Chennai

> "The audio guide at the City Palace was in Hindi and English. My wife understands neither. She stood there for 20 minutes and understood nothing. We left early." — Bengali traveler, Kolkata

> "We ended up calling our friend from Udaipur [AIESEC contact] to ask which hotel serves filter coffee. That's how we found it." — Tamil couple, Coimbatore

**Method 3: Online Community Research**
Analyzed 200+ posts in Tamil Nadu travel Facebook groups and Bengali travel communities (2014-2015) related to Rajasthan travel.

**Most common question types:**
1. "Any South Indian restaurants in [city]?" — 34% of posts
2. "Which hotel is safe/comfortable for elderly parents?" — 22%
3. "Any Tamil/Bengali speaking guides available?" — 18%
4. "Is filter coffee available anywhere in Jaipur/Udaipur?" — 14%
5. "Any events happening during [date] we should know about?" — 12%

**Insight:** The manual workaround already exists. People are asking these questions in Facebook groups. We are automating and scaling a behavior that already happens.

## Persona Profiles

---

### Persona 1: Karthik — "The Responsible Son"

**Demographics**
- Age: 36
- City: Chennai, Tamil Nadu
- Profession: Software Engineer, IT company
- Income: ₹12-18 LPA
- Travel frequency: 2-3 domestic trips per year
- Languages: Tamil (primary), English (work), Basic Hindi

**Trip Context**
Traveling to Udaipur for 5 days with wife (32) and parents (63, 66). Parents' first major trip outside Tamil Nadu. Karthik is responsible for all planning and logistics. High pressure to make the trip comfortable.

**Goals**
- Find South Indian / Tamil-friendly restaurants within walking distance of accommodation
- Book a hotel that serves South Indian breakfast (filter coffee, idli) — parents will be anxious otherwise
- Find a guide who can communicate clearly with his parents (Tamil or good English)
- Identify the 5-6 must-see sites and optimal sequence (parents tire easily)
- Have emergency contacts and practical information in Tamil

**Pain Points**
- TripAdvisor reviews are not from people who share his palate — high signal-to-noise problem
- Hotel websites don't mention food preferences at all
- Can't easily find Tamil-speaking guides online; must rely on word-of-mouth
- Parents feel excluded when signs, menus, guides are in Hindi/English they don't understand
- Planning took 6+ hours across multiple apps and Facebook groups

**Emotional Drivers**
- Wants to be seen as a great planner by his family
- Feels responsible for his parents' comfort and happiness
- Anxious about anything going wrong (medical, food, language barrier)
- Proud when he discovers something the Facebook group hasn't found yet

**Tech Behavior**
- Heavy smartphone user; uses apps for everything
- Trusts Google reviews moderately; highly trusts recommendations from Facebook travel groups
- Will install an app if the value proposition is immediately clear
- Expects app to work reliably; low tolerance for crashes or slow loads

**Quote:** "I spent more time planning this trip than I spent on my office project last month. And I'm still not sure if my parents will find familiar food."

### Persona 2: Meera — "The Cultural Explorer"

**Demographics**
- Age: 28
- City: Bangalore, Karnataka (originally from Mysore)
- Profession: UX Designer
- Income: ₹8-12 LPA
- Travel frequency: 4-5 domestic trips per year
- Languages: Kannada (primary), English (fluent), Basic Hindi

**Trip Context**
Solo trip to Udaipur for 4 days. Wants a mix of heritage exploration and authentic local food. Does not want tourist-trap experiences. Wants to find places the tourist crowd hasn't discovered. Comfortable with adventure and improvisation.

**Goals**
- Discover places reviewed by Kannada/South Indian travelers — trusts their taste
- Find authentic local events, not just the standard tourist circuit
- Try local Rajasthani food while also having backup South Indian options
- Document her trip for her travel blog; wants unique, authentic experiences

**Pain Points**
- Standard travel apps give the same recommendations to everyone — she wants differentiated discovery
- Can't find event listings in Kannada or English that include local, non-touristy events
- Wants genuine local guide, not a tourist-industry standard tour guide

**Quote:** "I want to eat where locals eat. But I also want to know if the food will destroy my stomach. Someone from South India who's been there can tell me that better than a foreign reviewer."

### Persona 3: Savitribai & Ramesh — "The First-Time Grandparents"

**Demographics**
- Ages: 64 (Savitribai), 67 (Ramesh)
- City: Patna, Bihar
- Languages: Bhojpuri (primary), Hindi (functional), No English
- Travel experience: Domestic only; mostly pilgrimage travel

**Trip Context**
Joining a group tour organized by their son (who lives in Udaipur). First time in Rajasthan. Anxious about unfamiliar environment, food, and language. High dependency on human guidance; very low smartphone proficiency.

**Goals**
- Find familiar North Indian vegetarian food (no Rajasthani spice level issues)
- Understand what they're seeing at heritage sites (Hindi explanations)
- Know that help is available if they need it
- Enjoy the experience without feeling lost or helpless

**Pain Points**
- Audio guides in English only — understand nothing
- Menus without Hindi descriptions — anxious about ordering
- Pharmacies and medical help — can't communicate if needed
- Feel like "second-class tourists" when young English-speaking tourists navigate confidently while they struggle

**Design Implication:** The app's Hindi mode must be designed for low-literacy users — larger text, icon-heavy navigation, simplified language. This persona uses the app with help from their son.

### Persona 4: Carlos — "The Diaspora Reconnector"

**Demographics**
- Age: 34
- City: Toronto, Canada (Tamil diaspora)
- Profession: Finance professional
- Languages: Tamil (home), English (primary), No Hindi

**Trip Context**
First visit to India in 8 years. Visiting family in Chennai and doing a 10-day Rajasthan leg solo. Unfamiliar with modern India, nervous about navigation, excited to explore.

**Goals**
- Navigate Udaipur without Hindi language barriers
- Find Tamil-friendly resources and community
- Authentic heritage experience with good English or Tamil explanation
- Safety-conscious: wants to understand what's safe, what to avoid

**Pain Points**
- Hindi-heavy infrastructure confusing for diaspora Tamil speaker
- Can't use Indian-localized apps easily (UPI, Indian phone number requirements)
- No familiarity with current local context

**Quote:** "I grew up in Toronto hearing about Rajasthan. Now I'm finally here and I can't understand half the signs. I feel more lost here than I do in Europe."

## Behavioral Segmentation

| Segment | % of Users (est.) | Primary Job | Retention Driver |
|---|---|---|---|
| Family trip planners (with elderly) | 40% | Comfort + safety | Guide booking, hotel filtering |
| Solo cultural explorers | 25% | Authentic discovery | Community reviews, event listings |
| First-time domestic tourists | 20% | Orientation + safety | Hindi-mode simplicity, offline content |
| Diaspora travelers | 10% | Cultural reconnection | Tamil/regional language UI |
| AIESEC/network-referred | 5% | Community participation | Review submission, content contribution |
